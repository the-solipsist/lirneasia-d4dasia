# Executive summary

**Data governance in the Philippines**

The Philippines data governance landscape is characterised by the
absence of an organised or comprehensive legal policy concerning data
flows or governance. Data does not easily fit into the traditional
property categories under the 1949 Civil Code, leading to a more casual
treatment compared to conventional property. Despite this, recent
decades have seen the enactment of relevant policies, such as the
Intellectual Property Code and the Data Privacy Act (DPA), and avowed
changes in public data management. The Philippines has also endorsed and
implemented the Open Government Declaration as a member of the Open
Government Partnership (OGP), and enacted laws like the 2007 National
Archives of the Philippines Act and an executive order for freedom of
information.

These changes, however, have not resulted in a unitary framework over
data; instead, legislation and rulemaking are confined to categories or
facets of data, driven by sector-specific concerns like personal data
protection or credit information. The 2016 presidential ordinance
governing requests for disclosure of government data based on the
constitutional right of access to information is the closest the
Philippines has to an overarching rule on data governance.

**Policies that increase access to data**

‘Public data’ refers to information gathered, processed, stored, or
generated by Philippines public authorities, intended to be
machine-readable. The Philippine Constitution mandates transparency and
disclosure of government records as a general rule, particularly through
Section 7, Article III (right to information on matters of public
concern) and Section 28, Article II (full public disclosure of
transactions involving public interest). However, these are subject to
limitations provided by law.

***Proactive state policies increasing access to data:***

- OGP: The Philippines, a founding member of OGP since 2011,
  institutionalised its commitments through Executive Order No. 31
  in 2023. Multiple government websites now provide public online access
  to data.

- Open Data Portal: Launched in 2014, this portal (data.gov.ph) serves
  as a central repository for public sector datasets from (as of the
  time of writing) 17 government departments and agencies, proactively
  releasing data such as statistics and key performance indicators.

- Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) OpenSTAT: PSA, the highest
  policymaking body on statistical matters, maintains a separate, more
  comprehensive public access platform (openstat.psa.gov.ph) for
  national and sub-national statistics under an open data license.
  Notably, despite PSA being listed as a contributor to the Open Data
  Portal, its contributions there are limited to annual reports,
  highlighting a lack of meaningful linkage between the two platforms.

- National government budget data: The Department of Budget and
  Management’s website and the PhilGEPS portal provide public access to
  budget data, procurement plans, and awarded contracts. All government
  agencies are also required to maintain a Transparency Seal on their
  websites, disclosing mandates, officials, budgets, procurement plans,
  major projects, and financial reports.

- Full disclosure policy portal for local government units: Since 2010,
  local governments have been required to make financial documents
  available online (via fdpp.dilg.gov.ph), with the Department of the
  Interior and Local Government (DILG) establishing a dedicated portal
  for this purpose.

- Advanced Science and Technology Institute (ASTI) Datasets: This
  platform (asti.dost.gov.ph/coare/datasets) hosts environmental and
  geospatial data for researchers and forecasters, aiming to enhance
  disaster management, though some datasets are not publicly available.

- National Archives of the Philippines (NAP): Established by the 1997
  National Archives Act, it governs public records with archival value,
  preserving them and making them available to the public. Records are
  classified as ‘pen access’ or ‘restricted’ after 30 years, with
  exceptions for national defense, security, privacy, and investigatory
  records. Public access remains primarily physical, with limited
  digitised material.

- Data formats and interoperability standards: The Philippines
  Electronic Government Interoperability Framework (PeGIF) was approved
  in 2014 to standardise data exchange. The 2016 Freedom of Information
  (FOI) Ordinance mandates government offices to maintain accurate
  records in appropriate formats. The DPA requires personal data
  controllers, including government, to implement security measures,
  with the National Privacy Commission (NPC) recommending ISO/IEC 27002
  and 27018 standards for government agencies and their service
  providers.

- Use of Free and Open-Source Software (FOSS): There is no official
  policy mandating FOSS, and proposed legislation has not advanced.

***Reactive government policies on allowing access:***

- Right to Information Ordinance (Executive Order No. 02, 2016): In the
  absence of an FOI statute, this EO operationalises the constitutional
  right to information within the executive branch. It establishes an
  administrative procedure for requests, requiring a written submission,
  assistance to the requester, a response within 15 working days, and an
  administrative appeal process for denials. An online portal was
  launched for this purpose.

- Civil and property records: The 1930 Law on Registry of Civil Status
  and the 1979 Property Registration Decree designate civil and land
  registration records as public documents, with access open to the
  public. A 2022 law mandates the Department of Information and
  Communications Technology (DICT) to develop a civil registry database.

- Land management data: The Land Management Bureau adopted a Land
  Administration and Management System (LAMS) in 2010 to ensure
  integrity and access to land information, though public access is not
  yet operational.

- Corporate and securities records: The Securities and Exchange
  Commission (SEC) regulates corporations, treating documents like
  articles of incorporation and annual financial statements as public
  records, viewable online for a fee via the SEC i-View system.

***Private sector mandates promoting openness:***

- Competition-related: The 2015 Philippines Competition Act enables the
  Philippines Competition Commission to access commercial documents
  related to significant merger and acquisition agreements to ensure
  compliance with competition laws.

- Credit information records: The 2008 Credit Information System Act
  (CISA) established a centralised system and created the Credit
  Information Corporation (CIC) to collect and disseminate credit
  information to lending institutions. CIC maintains strict
  confidentiality but allows lending entities and borrowers to access
  consolidated basic credit data.

***Policies that decrease openness***

Restrictions on data flow arise from contract stipulations or
legal/regulatory impositions. The 2016 Inventory of Exceptions to
Executive Order No. 02 lists broad exceptions to the right of access to
information, including executive privilege, national security, law
enforcement, privacy protection and information deemed confidential by
official capacity or specific laws (e.g., banking/finance).

***Decreasing openness to favor collective rights/common good:***

- Executive privilege or national security: The Supreme Court recognises
  executive privilege for presidential communications and policy
  formulation, and information related to national security, defense, or
  international relations. A 1964 Memorandum circular outlines
  confidentiality classifications for executive documents, each with
  specific rules on disclosure. The National Archives Act also allows
  access to be withheld on account of national defense/security,
  established privilege, investigatory records and financial institution
  stability.

- Indigenous data sovereignty: The Constitution and the 1997 Indigenous
  People’s Rights Act (IPRA) protect the rights of indigenous cultural
  communities (IPPs) to preserve and develop their cultures, traditions
  and institutions, granting them full ownership and control over their
  cultural and intellectual rights. Access to biological and genetic
  resources within ancestral lands requires Free and Prior Informed
  Consent (FPIC) from these communities.

- Data and collective privacy concerns: The DPA imposes obligations on
  data controllers, including government agencies, to protect personal
  data, with non-compliance incurring administrative and criminal
  penalties. Personal information must only be retained as long as
  necessary. The DICT’s Cloud First Policy generally permits no
  residency restrictions on government data in the cloud but applies
  restrictions for sensitive data, requiring storage within the
  Philippines or states with extradition treaties. Data sharing
  agreements between government agencies or for commercial purposes must
  be written and include safeguards.

- Disclosures related to money laundering statutes: The 2001 Anti-Money
  Laundering Act (AMLA) mandates covered persons (financial
  institutions, designated non-financial professions/businesses) to
  report covered and suspicious transactions to the Anti-Money
  Laundering Council (AMLC) electronically. Covered institutions must
  retain transaction records for at least five years, and ‘complex’
  entities are required to use electronic anti-money
  laundering/counter-terrorism financing (AML/CTF) systems.

- Establishing and maintaining government digitised databases: Recent
  laws increasingly mandate the collation of specific records into
  government-maintained databases, reflecting increased digitalisation,
  though not always for public access. Examples include the Philippines
  Identification System Act 2017 (also known as the PhilSys Act) for a
  national ID and PhilSys Registry, the Community-Based Monitoring
  System Act 2018 for localised poverty data, and various law
  enforcement databases (NBI, Anti-Terrorism Council, anti-trafficking,
  LTO for motorcycles, AMLC for money laundering transactions).

- SIM card registration information: The 2022 SIM Card Registration Act
  requires end-users to register their SIMs, but this information is
  treated as absolutely confidential, accessible to law enforcement only
  through a duly issued subpoena for crime investigation.

***Decreasing openness to favor individuals:***

- Protection of individual privacy rights: The DPA requires a lawful
  basis for processing personally identifying information (PII) and
  guaranteeing data subjects’ rights. PII must be retained only as long
  as necessary and disposed of securely. The DPA provides exemptions for
  PII processed for journalistic, artistic, literary, or research
  purposes (if for public benefit and under strict confidentiality).
  Other exemptions include information on matters of public concern
  (e.g. public officials’ functions, discretionary government benefits)
  and data necessary for public authority functions (e.g. law
  enforcement). Cross-border data transfers must adhere to DPA
  principles, ensuring comparable protection. Context-specific laws also
  impose privacy requirements (e.g. Philippines Statistical Act, SIM
  Card Registration Act, laws for vulnerable sectors).

- Promoting intellectual property rights: Copyright law protects
  literary, scholarly, scientific, and artistic works, including
  compilations of data. Fair-use exceptions permit use for criticism,
  news reporting, scholarship, research, and decompilation for
  interoperability. The Intellectual Property Code does not recognise
  AI-generated works as authors must be natural persons. Trade secrets
  and undisclosed information are also protected.

***Data privacy and security:***

- Online traffic data: The 2012 Cybercrime Prevention Act requires
  service providers to preserve traffic data for at least six months,
  accessible by law enforcement with a court warrant. The Supreme Court,
  in *Disini v. Secretary of Justice*, deemed real-time collection
  without a warrant, unconstitutional, due to privacy concerns.

- Data security measures: The DPA mandates appropriate organisational,
  physical, and technical measures for personal data protection by all
  controllers, including government and private entities. Government
  agencies are specifically guided by NPC Circular No. 16-01,
  recommending ISO/IEC 27002 and 27018 standards.

- Private sector mandates: The DICT’s Memorandum Circular No. 05 2017
  designates certain private sector entities as Critical Information
  Infrastructure (CII) (e.g. transportation, banking,
  telecommunications) and requires them to comply with ISO/IEC 27001,
  participate in security assessments, and establish Computer Emergency
  Response Teams (CERTs). DICT Circular No. 10 2020 extends the Cloud
  First Policy to private cloud service providers dealing with
  government data, imposing restrictions on data management, use,
  security and encryption key control.

- Exceptions to data security for law enforcement: The Constitution
  allows privacy invasion upon lawful court order or when public
  safety/order requires it. Specific laws permit interception of
  computer data with court warrants for cybercrime, terrorism and online
  sexual abuse of children. For the latter, undercover operations may
  proceed without a court order.

**Recommendations towards increasing data flows**

Despite the absence of a comprehensive data governance law, an existing
framework recognises the importance of information generation, the
constitutional right to access, and privacy. However, real-world
practices often frustrate data access and sharing. A significant
challenge is the absence of a unified/centralised framework for
inter-government data sharing and open access. The fragmented nature,
evidenced by the lack of integration between the Open Data Portal and
the more comprehensive PSA datasets, highlights poor inter-agency
coordination. Government agencies often show hesitancy in sharing data,
even for FOI requests, providing various justifications. The current FOI
Ordinance is deemed insufficient, with calls for a legislated FOI
statute to compel effective implementation and to revisit existing
exceptions that predate the constitutional right to information. The
lack of a clear data sharing mandate within government hinders
evidence-based policymaking. Executive action through an Executive Order
could mandate inter-agency sharing and revive initiatives like PeGIF,
which aimed for common data standards, but this indicates a low priority
within the executive branch.

The DPA is also frequently invoked to deny access, sometimes
inaccurately, even for non-identifying data or exempted categories. The
criminal penalties under the DPA make public officers overly
conservative in disclosing information, deterring access even when
warranted. Removing these criminal penalties, confining punishments to
administrative fines, could lead to more liberal information disclosure
by public custodians.

Another critical area for improvement is increasing access to traffic
data for law enforcement. The Supreme Court’s ruling in *Disini* that
made a judicial warrant a requirement for traffic data collection, has
hampered cybercrime investigations, as traffic data is often the first
step in identifying anonymous online criminals. The Budapest Convention
on Cybercrime, which the Philippines acceded to in 2018, distinguishes
traffic data from content data and subscriber information, requiring
‘expeditious disclosures’ of traffic data to identify service providers
and communication paths, without necessitating a judicial warrant (only
a ‘production order’). The current warrant requirement in the
Philippines, which conflates traffic, content and subscriber data, is
seen as burdensome and contrary to the Convention’s differentiated
treatment and call for expediency. Since the Supreme Court has exclusive
power to promulgate procedural rules, it could modify existing rules to
introduce a subpoena-like process for traffic data, balancing the
court’s concern for individual privacy with the Convention’s obligations
for expedited disclosure. Expediting access to traffic data is deemed a
necessity for comprehensive cybercrime investigations.

# Introduction

This report on data governance in **Philippines** is part of the
‘Harnessing Data for Democratic Development in South and Southeast Asia’
(D4DAsia) project, which aims, *inter alia*, to create and mobilise new
knowledge about tensions, gaps and the evolution of the data governance
ecosystem taking into account formal and informal policies and
practices.

In today’s digital age, data governance ecosystems play a crucial role
in shaping our societies. These ecosystems, comprising policies, laws,
practices, behaviours, and technologies, aim to govern data in ways that
protect rights, foster innovation, enhance transparency, and ultimately
promote democratic and inclusive governance. An ideal data governance
system would protect rights, enable innovation, improve transparency,
and help in bringing about democratic, inclusive governance. However,
the landscape of data governance is complex and often fraught with
challenges, particularly in South and Southeast Asia.

Through the rest of the report, unless the context indicates otherwise,
the term ‘policies’ is used as shorthand for policies, statutes,
regulations, rules, administrative orders and even practices and
technologies that are used to implement all of those as part of data
governance ecosystems.

Data is increasingly being recognised as an enabler for development. It
is an essential requirement for policymaking and monitoring of
development goals and targets. When effectively managed, data can be
used as an asset to support significant development actions such as
poverty reduction, food security, mitigating impact of climate change,
and disaster management. If mismanaged, it can exacerbate inequalities
and undermine the development potential of the same actions.

The D4DAsia project has produced nine reports so far: seven detailed
individual country reports that deal with the issues of data governance
in the following countries; India, Indonesia, Nepal, Pakistan,
Philippines, Sri Lanka and Thailand; a detailed look at data protection
in South Korea; and a synthesis report that summarises the findings from
the various countries while drawing out the contrasts amongst them,
along with detailed findings for the research questions we had posed.
The questions are:

1.  What is common, and what is nationally specific, in the emerging
    data governance architectures in South and Southeast Asia? What are
    the explanations?

2.  What are the implications of the emergent nature of the governance
    architecture? Because there is no overall design that envisions how
    the parts fit together, it is likely that there will be friction
    points and even contradictions. How are these being worked out?

3.  The emerging governance architecture involves trade-offs among
    objectives such as greater accountability of powerholders, economic
    growth, including the creation of employment and wealth, resilience
    of systems, etc. How have different societies: (a) explicitly
    recognised the trade-offs or not; and (b) handled them?

4.  Are there legislative or policy innovations with potential for
    replication? What are the modalities of sharing experiences? Are
    developing countries learning from each other, or are they learning
    from the developed countries?

5.  How were the laws and bills developed? What expertise was brought to
    bear? How open were the procedures? How receptive were drafters to
    suggestions and criticisms?

6.  How were capacity challenges addressed: by simplifying the laws or
    by tolerating incomplete implementation?

## Structure of the report

### Governance background

This report starts by providing contextual information about the
constitution and governance framework in **Philippines**, including how
lawmaking powers are distributed and delegated, the powers of the
judiciary to overturn laws or to enforce policies, and the legal and
regulatory background in the country.

### Increasing openness/access

The report then discusses policies that increase openness or access. By
this we mean policies that allow greater access by citizens, consumers,
and corporations to data, or facilitate interoperability or cross-border
data transfer. Specifically, we do not include increased governmental
access to citizens’ private data or non-public corporate data.

This section discusses open data policies, the question of how much
governmental data is made available proactively and how much is reactive
as well as the quality of data being disseminated. The report also
assesses government policies favouring or requiring free and open source
software (FOSS) or open standards, noting any specific standards that
are mandated.

### Decreasing openness/access

The report then moves on to discuss the opposite, i.e. laws, policies
and practices that decrease openness or access. By this we mean
decreasing access of citizens, consumers, and corporations to data. To
be clear, this is not a negative value judgement, since upholding
important individual and collective rights, such as privacy and public
security, necessitate reducing citizens’ access to data.

This theme explores issues of security such as whether there are any
data retention or localisation requirements, restrictions on the right
to access information (such as national security, privacy etc.) and
exceptions to data security requirements for law enforcement. We further
discuss the privacy and copyright framework in brief and specifically
try to answer whether there are any exceptions for search engines as
well as for research and artificial intelligence (AI).

The issue of data governance and the policies surrounding its
implementation is a critical one for governments, citizens and
businesses across the world. As mentioned earlier, we use the term data
governance to refer to ‘diverse arrangements, including technical,
policy, regulatory or institutional provisions, that affect data and
their creation, collection, storage, use, protection, access, sharing
and deletion across policy domains and organisational and national
borders.’[^1]

# Overview of the Philippine data governance landscape

The Philippines does not have an organised or a comprehensive legal
policy concerning data flows or data governance. Data does not easily
fit into the categories of property under the 1949 Civil Code, which
distinguishes only between real and personal property and further
categorises personal property as generally tangible objects.[^2] The
absence of unequivocal proprietary rights over data has resulted in a
more casual treatment of data as objects, in a manner that differs from
the more deliberate approach to more conventional types of property
where the legal ties that bind are more certain.

However, recent decades have seen the enactment of relevant data
governance policies that are more responsive to the increased uses and
flows of data in the private and public sector. For example, the
evolution of international agreements on intellectual property
protection have led to an intellectual property code more cognisant of
international practices and increased digitalisation; the increased
cross-border processing of personal information has led to a Philippine
law for the protection of personal data. Meanwhile, there have been
significant changes also as to the management and governance of public
data generated by the government or acquired in the exercise of
governmental functions. The Philippines has endorsed and implemented, to
some extent, the Open Government Declaration, which relates to the
promotion of ‘transparent, participatory, inclusive and accountable
governance’, as a member of the OGP.[^3] Other developments include the
enactment of the 2007 National Archives of the Philippines Act and an
executive order implementing the constitutional right to freedom of
information.[^4]

However, these changes have not resulted in, or do not imply the
existence of, a unitary framework policy on data governance. The closest
thing to an overarching rule is the 2016 Presidential Ordinance
governing requests for disclosure of government data in accordance with
the constitutional right of access to information. Otherwise, the
individual legislation or rule making that has emerged is confined to
particular categories or facets of data, animated by sector-specific
concerns, such as personal data protection or credit information.

This study shall detail the different policies, whether enacted by
Congress through statutes or by the President (often through Executive
Orders) and the various administrative agencies through their delegated
rule-making powers, governing the administration and transfer of data,
whether in the control of the private or public sector and whatever its
character or utility may be.

### Policies that increase access to data

‘Public data’, for purposes of this study, refers to data that is either
gathered, processed, stored, or generated by public authorities of the
Republic of the Philippines. ‘Data’ is further qualified as information
that is machine readable. Public data is distinguished from ‘privately
held data’, which refers to such information held or owned by the
private sector, but whose uses may nonetheless be regulated by the
government.

When it comes to public data, the Philippines has fundamental policies
as ordained under the Philippine Constitution that mandate transparency
and disclosure of government records and data as the general rule, even
as the Constitution itself accommodates the possibility of legislated
exceptions.[^5] The Bill of Rights, particularly Section 7, Article III
of the Constitution, guarantees the right of the people to information
on matters of public concern; however the same provision states that
people’s access to official records, to government research data used as
the basis for policy development and to documents and papers pertaining
to official acts, transactions, or decisions, may be subject to such
limitations as may be provided by law.

In a similar vein, while Section 28, Article II declares the adoption
and implementation of a policy of full public disclosure of all its
transactions involving public interest as state policy, it is still
subject ‘to reasonable conditions prescribed by law’.

Since the adoption of the Philippine Constitution in 1987, no law has
been passed by Congress establishing a mechanism for explicitly
implementing the constitutional right to access information ostensibly
guaranteed by the constitutional provisions. There are statutes such as
the 2007 National Archives Act that nonetheless govern the management
and administration of public records, with a thrust of ensuring their
preservation.[^6]

In 2016 an Executive Order (FOI Ordinance) issued by the President of
the Philippines, established an administrative procedure within the
executive branch of government for reviewing and granting requests for
access to government records and documents.[^7] This mechanism is
powered through an online portal and created a greater public
expectation that citizens may request government documents and data
online. The FOI Ordinance however, has not deterred government agencies
from withholding information that ostensibly does not fall within the
prescribed exceptions from disclosure.

In addition, there are different statutes and administrative rules that
enable access by the public of certain types of publicly and privately
held data that, owing to public interest or pursuant to any public
purpose, has been required to be submitted to a specific government
agency or instrumentality and/or disclosed to the public.

#### Proactive state policies increasing access to data/content

The Philippine Constitution, particularly Article II, Section 28,
requires that the ‘State adopts and implements a policy of full public
disclosure of all its transactions involving public interest subject to
reasonable conditions prescribed by law’.[^8] However, this provision,
unlike Article III, Section 7, falls within the general declarations of
state policies that, under Philippines jurisprudence, generally, are not
by themselves the source of legal rights or obligations that are
enforceable by the courts. Still, the constitutionally ordained policies
are expected to guide legislators and policymakers in crafting rules and
regulations. And in recent years, government transparency has indeed
become part of the public-facing aspects of state governance, especially
with the emergence of the internet space as a portal for interaction
between the government and the governed.

Article II, Section 28 provides sufficient legal guidance to the
Philippine government as it determines its specific policies on public
disclosure of government-held data. While it could also be invoked as
the basis for disclosure by the government of unreleased data through an
adversarial proceeding, it is preferable to obtain the data without
having to resort to such a contentious process.

Even before the internet era, there was already an established doctrine
that mandated the non-discretionary publication in either the Official
Gazette or in a newspaper of general circulation of all statutes, and of
all administrative rules and regulations if their purpose is to enforce
or implement existing law pursuant also to a valid delegation.[^9]
Further, the 1987 Administrative Code also required that every rule
promulgated by an administrative agency be filed with the University of
the Philippines Law Center as a condition for such rule becoming the
basis for any sanction against any party or persons.[^10] The compulsory
force of these holdings does not extend to such government
information/data that is not in the form of a rule or regulation.
However, the constitutional policy of transparency, coupled with the
emergence of the internet as a public-facing communication platform at
the disposal of the government, has resulted in the pro-active voluntary
release of a considerable volume of government data.

A mandated policy of government disclosure also necessarily requires a
policy of preservation of such covered data. The recent advent of data
privacy policies, with their emphasis on limited data sharing and
retention dictated strictly by need, may have driven holders of
government data towards lesser access.[^11]

##### Open government data under auspices of open government partnership

The Philippines is among the eight founding members of OGP, a broad
international coalition of 75 countries formed in 2011 that aims to
promote increased access to information and disclosure about
governmental activities at every level of government.[^12][^13] In June
2023, the Philippines’ government institutionalised its commitments
under OGP through the promulgation of Executive Order No. 31, which
established a Steering Committee that sets the direction, programmes and
activities of PH-OGP.[^14] The Executive Order also committed to the
establishment of a national action plan to formulate programmes and
policies that conform to the OGP’s values.

At present, there are several government websites maintained by
different agencies that enable public online access to government data,
consistent with the OGP’s mission.

##### Open data portal

In 2014, the Philippine government launched the Open Data Portal[^15] as
part of the government’s programme to proactively release public sector
datasets and generate an ecosystem for its use by the public. The portal
serves as a repository for open datasets from various Philippine
government agencies. The data available on the portal pertains to the
results of their operations, projects and the performance of their
respective mandates such as statistics and key performance indicators.
The portal is maintained by the DICT, with policy direction overseen by
an Open Data Task Force consisting of 10 government agencies.

At present, 17 government departments and agencies contribute to the
Open Data Portal. This includes cabinet-level departments such as the
Departments of Agriculture, Education, Foreign Affairs, Health,
Information and Communications Technology, Labor and Employment, and
Transportation, and law enforcement agencies such as the Philippine
National Police Anti-Cybercrime Group and the Bureau of Jail Management
and Penology. PSA is also listed as a contributor to the Open Data
Portal, even though it also maintains a separate open data platform for
the statistical data that it collates.

##### Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) OpenSTAT

The PSA was established in 2013 through the 2013 Philippines Statistical
Act.[^16] The PSA is the highest policy-making body on statistical
matters. As such, it is primarily responsible for all national censuses
and surveys, sectoral statistics, consolidation of selected
administrative recording systems and compilation of national accounts.
The PSA is required to collect, compile, analyse, abstract, and publish
statistical information relating to the country’s economic, social,
demographic, political affairs and general activities and condition of
the people. The PSA was also tasked in 2017 as the primary implementing
agency to carry out the 2017 Philippines Identification System Act, the
law that established a single national identification system for all
citizens and resident aliens of the Philippines.[^17]

The PSA is required further to collaborate with departments of the
national government in the collection, compilation, maintenance and
publication of statistical information, including special statistical
data derived from the activities of those departments, corporations, and
their subsidiaries. Another relevant duty of the PSA is to promote and
develop integrated social and economic statistics and coordinate plans
for the integration of those statistics, including the national
accounts. While these mandates clearly align with the purpose of the
Open Data initiative, the PSA does not appear to be actively involved
with that initiative, especially in a public-facing manner. Instead, it
maintains a separate public access online platform known as OpenSTAT
\[“PSA OpenSTAT \> Home”;[^18] see also openstat.psa.gov.ph\], which
contains the statistics generated and compiled by the PSA at the
national and sub-national levels, organised into three major
domains—demographics and social statistics, economic statistics, and
environment and multi-domain statistics.

The data in the OpenSTAT platform is available under an open data
license which allows for the use of the data without restrictions, save
for proper source attribution. The data made available online by the PSA
includes summarised and/or aggregated data, as well as microdata that is
available on a separate webpage.[^19]

A review of the PSA’s OpenSTAT platform reveals that the included
datasets are more comprehensive and more substantive than those that are
available on the Open Data Portal. Owing to the agency’s statutory
mandate that requires it to focus on data gathering and analysis, it is
not surprising that the management of the OpenSTAT platform is more
professional as compared to the Open Data Portal. The Open Data Portal
unfortunately has proven thus far unable to take advantage or make any
meaningful linkages to the PSA database. As earlier mentioned, the PSA
is listed under the Open Data website as among the agencies that have
contributed datasets to that portal. However, an examination of the
actual dataset reveals that the PSA contribution is limited to the PSA’s
annual reports from 2012 to 2021, reports which are all also available
on the PSA website. These annual reports are glossy publications that
almost exclusively focus on the PSA’s activities and accomplishments for
the given year, as opposed to a compilation of data actually gathered
and processed by the agency. Any reference to actual statistical data in
these reports consists of a few pages of infographics that highlight
some data of general public interest, such as birth, death and marriage
statistics.

##### National government budget data

Philippine government national budget data is publicly available through
the Department of Budget and Management’s official website.[^20] The
website also includes documents related to public bidding and
procurement plans. Government procurement is controlled by the
Government Procurement Reform Act, which requires a single online portal
(known as PhilGEPS) serving as the primary source on all government
procurement, and the facility by which all government entities are to
procure common supplies.[^21]

All agencies of the Philippine government are also required to maintain
a Transparency Seal posted in their websites which contain the following
data:

- the agency’s mandates and functions, names of its officials with their
  position and designation and contact information

- approved budgets and corresponding targets, including any
  modifications made;

- annual procurement plans and awarded contracts;

- major activities or projects and their target beneficiaries;

- status of implementation, evaluation or assessment reports of the
  agency’s programmes or projects;

- the agency’s budget and financial accountability reports;

- the agency’s FOI manual signed; and

- annual reports on the status of income authorised by law to be
  retained or used and be deposited outside of the National
  Treasury.[^22]

Thus, even if an agency’s budget data is not part of the larger datasets
under the Open Data Portal, such information is required to be available
in the agency’s own website.

##### Full disclosure policy portal for local government units

Local government units of the Philippines were, under the 1991 Local
Government Code, specifically required to post a summary of all revenues
collected and funds received, including the appropriations and
disbursement of such funds during the fiscal year.[^23] The summary had
to be posted within 30 days from the end of each fiscal year in at least
three publicly accessible and conspicuous places in the local government
unit. In 2010, the Department of Interior and Local Government (DILG)
required local governments to make available online, whether through
their own websites or through the website of the department, financial
documents including annual budgets, cash flows, fund utilisation and
bidding information.[^24] DILG has likewise established a Full
Disclosure Policy Portal where such local government information may be
accessed.[^25]

##### Advanced Science and Technology Institute (ASTI) datasets

ASTI, an institute under the Department of Science and Technology, hosts
a platform that serves as a repository for environmental and geospatial
data,[^26] available to researchers, meteorologists, weather forecasters
and students, with a stated goal of providing enhanced policy and
decision making for disaster management. Access to the datasets is free,
but some of the uploaded datasets are not made available to the public.
There are 14 datasets currently available on the platform, including
storm surge hazard maps, landslide hazard maps and a drought situation
map. The storm surge and landslide maps were created by the University
of the Philippines through its own Project NOAH (Nationwide Operational
Assessment of Hazards) initiative and are likewise available through its
own website hosted by the university.

##### National Archives of the Philippines (NAP)

The 1997 National Archives Act governs all public records with archival
value, as well as archival and records management programmes and
activities in all branches of government.[^27] The law established the
NAP, where public archives are stored, preserved, conserved and made
available to the public.[^28] Archives refer to public records, papers,
periodicals, books or other materials, whether electronic, audio-visual,
or print, which by their nature and characteristics have enduring value
and have been selected for permanent preservation.

Under the Act, when public records have been in existence for 30 years
or more, or are about to be transferred to the control of NAP’s
executive director, the records are to be classified as either ‘open
access’ or ‘restricted’ by the government agency that had been in
control of the records. The classification of such records as
‘restricted access’ is premised on either the existence of a legal
impediment to their disclosure, or there being a standard or advice
issued by the NAP executive director that requires such public records
be withheld from public access.

The 2009 Implementing Rules to the National Archives Act provides that
the NAP shall provide official information, records, or documents to any
requesting public, subject to the following exceptions:

- national defense or security matters;

- documents that place the life and safety of an individual in imminent
  danger;

- documents falling within the concept of established privilege, as may
  be established by law or jurisprudence;

- drafts of decisions, orders, rulings, policies, decisions, memoranda,
  and like documents;

- investigatory records that interfere with enforcement proceedings,
  deprive one a right to fair trial or adjudication, disclose the
  identity of a confidential source, or unjustifiably disclose
  investigative techniques and procedures; and

- information whose premature disclosure endangers the stability of a
  financial institution or frustrates the implementation of a proposed
  official action.

The NAP is further required to protect the right to privacy of
individuals, groups and organisations, including records, protocols, and
policies against indiscriminate usage.

The primary role of the National Archives Act in data governance is in
ensuring the administration of preserved government records. The website
of NAP is not a portal that enables public access to archival material;
it does not contain a general repository of digitised archival material
even as it informs the public of the list of collections it maintains.
Public access to archival material is still primarily through physical
access, thus ensuring that while data is accessible, it is not in a
format or through a mechanism that promotes its widespread
dissemination. Nonetheless, the website highlights certain archival
material from the era of Spanish colonisation that has been digitised;
it is conceivable that the amount of digitised archival material will
increase in the future. However, no specific prescribed policies for the
digitalisation of such materials or the enabling of wider public access
have been released by NAP.

##### Data formats and interoperability standards

In 2014, the Information and Communications Technology Office of the
Department of Science and Technology issued circulars approving PeGIF
Version 1.0 for implementation by government agencies. PeGIF was
intended to define the common knowledge, principles, and standards that
national government agencies were to use in the design of data and
information systems to enable data exchange and reuse across
agencies.[^29] PeGIF promised to implement specifications that comply
with the definition of open standards, contribute to open systems, and
are relevant to systems’ interconnectivity, data integration,
presentation and interface, e-services access and content management
metadata.[^30]

A succeeding ICTO-DOST circular pertained to information
interoperability, defined as ‘the ability to transfer and use
information in a uniform and effective manner across multiple
organisations and information technology systems… ensuring that
organisations share a common understanding and meaning for information
that is shared across different IT systems.’[^31] The circular states
that information interoperability is achieved reliably through the use
of controlled terminologies, and controlled mapping-tables and
mapping-rules for any transformation. Among the stated operating
principles in the circular are the use by government of a common
language and standardised approach to information exchange, and the use
of whole-of-government tools to ensure consistency in information
management practices and the improvement of agencies’ ability to reuse
information.

The responsibility for implementing PeGIF was assigned to the ICTO-DOST,
with its Deputy Executive Director for eGovernment to take the lead in
the adoption of standard definitions and formats for information
sharing, and the production of information interoperability assets.

In 2015, the ICTO-DOST was abolished by the law creating DICT, with its
functions and powers transferred to the new executive department.[^32]
DICT then became the executive department tasked as the primary policy
and implementing entity in developing the national ICT agenda.[^33]

Although the ICTO-DOST was abolished, the PeGIF circulars remain in
force; the DICT has not formally repealed them. However, the DICT has
made no visible effort to implement PeGIF or carry out the tasks set out
in the circulars, such as creating standard definitions and formats for
information sharing.

In a broadly similar vein, Section 14 of Executive Order No. 02 (2016
FOI Ordinance) mandates government offices to create and/or maintain
accurate and reasonably complete records of important information in
appropriate formats, and implement a records management system that
facilitates easy identification, retrieval and communication of
information to the public,[^34] as a means to operationalise the
constitutional right to information and state policies to ensure full
public disclosure and transparency in the public service.[^35]

The DPA requires personal data controllers, including the government, to
implement reasonable and appropriate organisational, physical, and
technical measures intended for the protection of personal data. For
government agencies that control the processing of personal information,
the NPC has issued NPC Circular No. 16-01 governing security of personal
data in government agencies. Section 6 of the circular recommends the
use of the ISO/IEC 27002 control set as the minimum standard to assess
any gaps in the agency’s control framework \[ISO/IEC 27002 is an
international standard that provides guidance on the establishment,
implementation and improvement of and Information Security Management
System; see *ISO/IEC 27002*[^36]\]. Section 12 of the circular states
that the commission recommends ISO/IEC 27018 as the most appropriate
certification for the service or function provided by a service provider
that is engaged, either for the storage of data or as a personal
information processor, acting under the control of the agency.[^37]

##### Use of free and open-source software

There is no official policy requiring the use of FOSS by the government.
Legislations that seek to require the use and adoption of FOSS by the
government have been filed with the Congress in the past, but no such
bills are being deliberated upon by the current Congress. The proposed
blueprint for a harmonised government information system developed under
the previous Duterte administration, known as the e-Government
Masterplan, acknowledged that the framework for ‘e-Government systems
and applications’ includes ‘encouraging an open-source software
development framework’; however, this has not been translated into
definite rules or policy regulations issued under the current
administration.[^38]

#### Reactive government policies on allowing access

The proactive data disclosure policies thus far adopted by the
Philippine government rely on the sole discretion of the government as
to what government data should be made available. Still, such proactive
policies should be seen in conjunction with more reactive policies that
enable the release of government information upon legal demand.

While proactive policies in the Philippines have a constitutional
foundation in the state policy of transparency, reactive policies also
draw upon the Constitution as their foundation, but this time through
the Bill of Rights (Article III), particularly Section 7, which as
mentioned earlier, guarantees citizens, ‘access to official records, and
to documents and papers pertaining to official acts, transactions, or
decisions, as well as to government research data used as basis for
policy development’ though subject to such limitations as may be
provided by law.

This right to access information on matters of public concern is
guaranteed by the Philippine Constitution and is enforceable against the
State, albeit subject to statutory and jurisprudential limitations.
Several provisions of the Philippine Constitution recognise and
facilitate access to information. The most potent of these provisions is
located in the Bill of Rights (Article III), Section 7 as stated above,
which also explicitly recognises ‘the right of the people to information
on matters of public concern’.

##### Right to information ordinance

The constitutional right to access information on matters of public
concern is, as indicated in Article III, Section 7, also subject to
limitations as may be provided by law. However, the ability of Congress
to impose limitations by law does not impair the enforceability of the
right before Philippine courts. The Philippine Supreme Court has
affirmed the provision as the basis for requiring the disclosure of
information pertaining to government housing loans extended to members
of the legislature,[^39] about successful bidders in public
infrastructure projects,[^40] and details about the equipment and
devices to be used in automated national elections.[^41] It has been the
basis for the Philippine Supreme Court to enjoin the President from
barring executive officials from giving testimony before legislative
committees.[^42]

The Philippine legislature has yet to enact a statute that would
administer the right to information specified under Article III, Section
7; thus there are no legislative dictates that clarify the procedures
for, or limitations on, how an individual may invoke the right. While
the absence of such a FOI law does not deter the judicial enforcement of
the right, it results in the efficacy of the right being greatly
dependent on executive discretion, as well as the motivation of
potential litigants, especially since a resort to the courts entails a
potentially laborious and adversarial process that hardly facilitates
access.

Nonetheless, in 2016, the Philippine government enacted Executive Order
No. 02 (2016 FOI Ordinance) with the intention of operationalising
within the executive branch the constitutional right to information, the
President doing so in his capacity as the administrative head of the
executive.[^43] In the absence of an FOI statute, Executive Order No. 02
is the most important Philippine ordinance concerning the right to
information. It establishes an administrative procedure for the filing
and processing of requests for access to information, public records,
and official records. The procedure, detailed in Section 9, is as
follows:

- a written request is submitted to the government office concerned,
  containing the name and contact information of the requesting party,
  valid proof of identification or authorisation, a reasonably specific
  description of the information requested, and the reason for, or
  purpose of, the request for information.

- The public official receiving the request shall provide reasonable
  assistance, free of charge, to enable all requesting parties and
  particularly those with special needs, to satisfy their request.

- The government office shall respond to a request as soon as
  practicable but not exceeding 15 working days from receipt thereof.
  The response mentioned above refers to the decision of the agency or
  office concerned to grant or deny access to the information requested.
  The period to respond may be extended whenever the information
  requested requires extensive search of the government office’s records
  facilities or examination of voluminous records.

- Once a decision is made to grant the request, the person making the
  request shall be notified of such decision and directed to pay any
  applicable fees for reimbursement of necessary costs, including actual
  costs of reproduction and copying of the information required.

- Denials of access requests may be appealed within the administrative
  branch, a process which generally extends up to final administrative
  review by the Office of the President. Only upon the exhaustion of
  administrative appeal remedies may the requesting party file an
  appropriate case with the proper courts.

Significantly, even though the Executive Order was silent as to the
utilisation of the internet in dealing with requests for information
(and indeed refers to an offline process), the government launched an
online portal through which such requests can be submitted and documents
released in November 2016.[^44] The portal is available at
(http://www.foi.gov.ph/. As of September 2023, a total of 185,727 eFOI
requests were lodged through the portal, of which 112,769 were tagged as
successful.

Executive Order No. 02 likewise directed every government office to
prepare their own People’s FOI Manual, which publicly identifies, among
others, the procedure for filing of access to information requests, the
person or office responsible for receiving requests, and the process for
the disposition of the requests. In compliance, Philippine government
offices have their own individual FOI Manuals. As a result, every
government agency has their own ordained procedure for handling requests
for access to information within their control.

##### Civil and property records

The 1930 Law on Registry of Civil Status (Act No. 3753) remains the
basic law governing the civil register that records the civil status of
persons, including births, deaths, marriages, and changes of name, all
of which must be recorded with the local civil registrar.[^45] Such
registration requirements are also reiterated in Articles 407 and 408 of
the 1949 Civil Code.[^46] Every city and municipality has their own
local civil registrar who keeps their own books that record the civil
status of persons. The books making up the civil register and all
documents relating thereto are considered public documents, access to
which is required to be open to the public during office hours.
Certified copies of such documents may be issued by the local registrar,
or by the PSA. Following the enactment of Republic Act No. 11909 in
2022, DICT was mandated to develop a civil registry database and virtual
viewing facility that would allow the easier verification of the
authenticity of such civil registry records.[^47]

The registration of private land ownership is currently governed by the
1979 Property Registration Decree, which mandates the registration of
certificates of title confirming land ownership, with the issuance of
such certificates the function of Registers of Deed in every province
and city.[^48] The office of the Register of Deeds is a public
repository of records of instruments such as deeds of absolute sale
affecting registered or unregistered lands, with such instruments
required to be registered with the Register of Deeds. Section 56 of the
Property Registration Decree provides that all records and papers
relative to registered and (including such private instruments of
transfer) be ‘open to the public in the same manner as court records,
subject to such reasonable regulations as the Register of Deeds may
prescribe’. In practice, each individual Register of Deeds is
functionally able to act with a high degree of autonomy in prescribing
regulations for their respective offices, resulting in varied practices
across provinces and cities.

##### Land management data

The Philippines’ Land Management Bureau adopted LAMS in 2010.[^49] LAMS
is an ‘enhanced land record management facility that ensures integrity
of and access to land information such as cadastral maps, isolated
survey plans, public land applications, and patents and titles.’[^50]
The issuance which formalises the adoption of the system adheres to the
policy that the Land Management Bureau shall ensure maximum access to
land information by both the government and the public.[^51] Notably,
while LAMS is operational within the various offices of the Land
Management Bureau, with access granted to the various Registers of Deed
in cities and municipalities, the promised access to the public has yet
to be operationalised.

##### Corporate and securities records

The 2019 Revised Corporation Code currently provides for the regulatory
jurisdiction of SEC over all corporations.[^52] The SEC oversees the
registration of a corporation and is also the body to which the
reporting requirements of corporations required under the Code are to be
submitted. The Code requires the annual submission by every corporation
doing business in the Philippines, whether domestic or foreign, of
annual financial statements and a general information sheet. SEC treats
a corporation’s articles of incorporation, by-laws, and related
documents as public records, which are available to the public.[^53] The
SEC has an online viewing system, the SEC i-View, which allows the
public to view and print such company reports for a fee.[^54]

#### Private sector mandates promoting openness

Legislation or administrative rules have also, on occasion, resulted in
regulations that would require the submission to government agencies of
private documents with the intention of making such documents or data
generated therefrom accessible to authorised persons, whether in the
government or even in the private sector.

##### Competition-related: open marketplaces

The enforcement of Philippines competition law, particularly the 2015
Philippines Competition Act, enables the Philippines Competition
Commission (PCC) to gain access to certain commercial documents and
records relevant to merger or acquisition agreements where the value of
the transaction exceeds one billion pesos (P1,000,000,000).[^55] These
agreements cannot be concluded by the parties until 30 days after
providing notification to the PCC in the form and with information on
the transaction, together with documents such as copies of the
preliminary agreement, non-compete agreements, studies and reports
related to the transaction, confidential information memoranda, and
banker’s books and third-party documents[^56] This requirement is in
line with PCC’s mandate to review proposed mergers and acquisitions to
ensure that they do not violate Philippines competition laws.[^57]

##### Transparency-oriented: credit information records

Through CISA, the Philippines established ‘a comprehensive and
centralised credit information system for the collection and
dissemination of fair and accurate information relevant to, or arising
from, credit and credit-related activities of all entities participating
in the financial system.’[^58] This enables lending institutions such as
banks, to more comprehensively assess the credit-worthiness of a
prospective borrower through the borrower’s basic credit data, or the
‘positive and negative information provided by a borrower \[in\]
connection with the application for and availment of a credit facility
and any information on the borrower’s creditworthiness in the possession
of the submitting entity and other factual and objective information
related or relevant thereto in the submitting entity’s data files or
that of other sources of information.’[^59] The specific information
used to assess a borrower’s basic credit data is listed in the
Implementing Rules and Regulations of the CISA.[^60]

CISA established a state-owned corporation, the Credit Information
Corporation (CIC), with the mandate to receive and consolidate basic
credit data, act as the central repository of credit information and
provide access to reliable, standardised information on credit history
and financial condition of borrowers.[^61] Under current procedures, CIC
requires the lending institutions to collect the information comprising
the basic credit data from the borrower itself, with CIC then processing
the data into the basic form. Entities involved in the process,
including CIC, submitting entities[^62] and those with access to
information are required to hold credit information under strict
confidentiality.[^63] Meanwhile, CIC is authorised to release and
disclose consolidated basic credit data only to specific entities[^64]
such as accessing entities,[^65] special accessing entities,[^66]
outsource entities[^67] and borrowers.[^68] The borrower, by having
access to the consolidated basic credit data, can dispute the assessment
given to it.

The researchers interviewed the current Executive Director of CISA,[^69]
who has confirmed the implementation of the current processes as CISA is
authorised to undertake under the law and implementing rules.
Additionally, he explained that he had expressed his willingness to make
financial data available for analysis to other government financial
authorities for the implementation of their own mandates, subject to the
anonymisation of irrelevant personally identifying data.

### Policies that decrease openness

Data flow restrictions could arise through private contract stipulations
(such as confidentiality agreements), or through impositions prescribed
by regulations. This next section focuses on the latter
scenario—restrictions that arise by operation of law.

The Office of the President in 2016 provided for a summary of the
exceptions to the right of access to information as recognised by the
Constitution, existing laws, or jurisprudence. These exceptions,
broadly, are:

1.  information covered by executive privilege;

2.  privileged information relating to national security, defense, or
    international relations;

3.  information concerning law enforcement and protection of public and
    personal safety;

4.  information deemed confidential for the protection of privacy of
    certain individuals such as minors, victims of crimes or the
    accused;

5.  information, documents, or records known by reason of official
    capacity and deemed as confidential, including those submitted or
    disclosed by entities to government agencies, tribunals and boards
    or officers, in relation to the performance of their functions or to
    inquires or investigation conducted by them in the exercise of their
    administrative, regulatory or quasi-judicial powers;

6.  prejudicial, premature disclosure;

7.  records of proceedings or information from proceedings, which,
    pursuant to law or relevant rules and regulations, are treated as
    confidential or privileged;

8.  matters considered confidential under banking and finance laws and
    their amendatory laws, and;

9.  other exceptions to the right to information under laws,
    jurisprudence, and rules and regulations.

The above-mentioned exceptions are listed in a Memorandum from the
Executive Secretary dated 24 November 2016, also known as Inventory of
Exceptions to Executive Order No. 2 (S. 2016).[^70] Executive Order
No. 2, or the FOI Ordinance, had directed the preparation of an
inventory of exceptions to access to information based on such
exceptions ‘enshrined in the Constitution, existing law or
jurisprudence’. The justifications for such exceptions align with
categories favouring collective rights or the common good, and those
required for the protection of individual rights. Whatever the
justification is, the enforceability of such restrictions to information
access requires an underlying law or regulation enacted by Congress or
executive agencies, or a judicial doctrine pronounced by the Supreme
Court, judicial decisions in the Philippines applying or interpreting
the laws or the Constitution forming a part of the legal system of the
Philippines.[^71] Thus, the government stands at risk of violating laws
and incurring legal liability in exercising discretion in making data
available in defiance of these exceptions as legislated or as judicially
defined.

#### Decreasing openness to favor collective rights/common good

##### Justified by executive privilege/national security

The Philippine Supreme Court has recognised the doctrine of executive
privilege that allows for certain government information to be withheld
from public disclosure. Such information may not be disclosed even upon
the issuance of a subpoena by a judicial or legislative body. The
President of the Philippines and their close advisors may withhold
information about presidential conversations, correspondence,
discussions in closed-door meetings, and information and records
comprising part of a process by which government decisions and policies
are formulated, including drafts of decisions, orders, rulings, and
policy decisions.

The Philippine Supreme Court has also recognised that information may
also be protected from disclosure if these relate to national security,
defense, or international relations. Diplomatic negotiations and other
information required to be kept secret in the conduct of foreign affairs
falls within this category. Information, records, or documents
classified as ‘secret’ in the interest of national security are also
exempted from disclosure.

The governing regulation for the classification of executive documents
and records as ‘secret’ is a 1964 Memorandum Circular issued by the
Office of the President.[^72] Under the circular, official matters
requiring protection in the interest of national security are limited to
four categories of classification, each with its own level of
accessibility.[^73]

- **Top Secret** matters (i.e., the unauthorised disclosure of which
  would cause exceptionally grave damage to the nation, politically,
  economically, or from a security aspect) may only be copied,
  extracted, or reproduced when the classifying authority (i.e. the Head
  of the Department) has authorised such action.[^74] Top secret matters
  are not allowed to be transmitted by mail, express or electrical
  means, unless in cryptographic form.[^75]

- **Secret** matters (i.e. the unauthorised disclosure of which would
  endanger national security, cause serious injury to the interest or
  prestige of the nation or of any governmental activity, or would be of
  great damage to a foreign nation), limitations on whose reproduction
  are similar to those on top secret matters.[^76]

- **Confidential** matters (i.e. the unauthorised disclosure of which,
  while not endangering national security, would be prejudicial to the
  interest or prestige of the nation or any government activity, or
  would cause administrative embarrassment or unwarranted injury to an
  individual or would be of advantage to a foreign nation), whose
  reproduction is generally authorised, except when the originator or a
  higher authority has specifically denied it.[^77]

- **Restricted** matters (i.e., those which require special protection
  other than that determined to be Top Secret, Secret, or Confidential),
  whose reproduction is authorised.[^78] In this regard, government
  personnel are prohibited from keeping a private record containing
  statements of facts or opinions, either official or personal,
  concerning matters which are related to or which affect national
  interest or security.[^79]

As earlier noted, under the National Archives Act, NAP does not grant
access to official information, records or documents to the requesting
public for, among others: (1) national defense or security matters;
established privilege matters; (3) investigatory records; (4) matters
that endanger the stability of financial institutions.[^80] Such
determinations, under that law, are made by the Executive Director of
the National Archives.

Philippine laws also criminalise certain acts of dissemination of
information such as espionage, correspondence with a hostile country
during times of war and some disloyal acts or works in time of peace
(e.g. distribution of information which advises or urges
insubordination, disloyalty, mutiny, or refusal of duty).[^81]

##### Justified by indigenous data sovereignty

The Philippine Constitution recognises, respects and protects the rights
of indigenous cultural communities to preserve and develop their
cultures, traditions and institutions.[^82] The principal law governing
indigenous people’s rights is the 1997 Indigenous People’s Rights Act, a
law that entitles indigenous peoples of the Philippines[^83] (IPPs) to
full ownership and control over their cultural and intellectual
rights[^84] in communal fashion, such community intellectual rights
forming part of their right to cultural integrity, which they have the
right to own, control, develop and protect.[^85]

The said law recognises the inherent rights of IPPs to self-governance
and self-determination with regard to their values, practices and
institutions.[^86] To operationalise the state policy to respect,
recognise, and protect the right of IPPs to preserve and protect their
culture, traditions and institutions, the law establishes that IPPs have
the right to practice and revitalise their own cultural traditions and
customs, as well as the right to restitution of cultural, intellectual,
religious and spiritual property taken without their free and prior
informed consent or in violation of their laws, traditions and
customs.[^87] Having full ownership and control over their cultural and
intellectual rights, IPPs have the right to special measures to control,
develop and protect their sciences, technologies and cultural
manifestations, including derivatives of these resources.[^88] With
regard to biological and genetic resources, access is allowed within
ancestral lands and domains of the Indigenous Cultural Communities/IPPs
only with the free and prior informed consent of such communities.[^89]

The National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP), in conjunction
with the Intellectual Property Office, issued rules and regulations on
intellectual property rights application and registration protecting the
indigenous knowledge systems and practices of Indigenous Cultural
Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPPs).[^90] Under these rules, the
right to indigenous knowledge systems and practices is an inherent part
of their cultural patrimony, which ICCs/IPPs have the right to regulate
access for research and documentation and which they have the right to
own, control, develop and protect.[^91] Although the guidelines were
issued in relation to large scale/extractive/intrusive activities, NPC
has considered the guidelines in obtaining consent from ICCs.[^92]

The Philippines has particular controls on the prospecting of biological
and genetic resources for scientific and commercial purposes. Executive
Order No. 247, promulgated by the Philippine President in 1995,
establishes a regulatory framework for such prospecting of biological
and genetic resources, as well as their by-products and
derivatives.[^93] If the prospecting were to be within the ancestral
lands and domains of ICCs, the prior informed consent of such
communities must be obtained, in accordance with the customary laws of
the concerned communities. The executive order requires the prior
informed consent of the concerned local communities even if the
prospecting does not involve ICCs.

An incident in 2021 illustrates the degree of control that IPPs maintain
under the law over their own data—their genetic data in particular.
Following the publication of a landmark study tracing the genetic
history of the Filipino people that had relied on 1,094 individual
saliva samples sourced from various IPP communities, it was revealed
that the researcher who oversaw the study had not secured the necessary
clearances from the NCIP. This was characterised by NPC in an advisory
opinion as a failure to secure the requisite informed consent from the
IPPs, notwithstanding the researcher’s claims that they had secured the
consent directly from the subjects.[^94] NPC concluded that the failure
to comply with the procedural and documentary requirements on consent as
prescribed by the NCIP resulted in invalid informed consent as required
under the Indigenous People’s Rights Act.[^95] Despite the
pronouncement, the advisory opinion did not impose any sanctions or
award damages on account of the violation.

##### Privacy and data concerns

The DPA is a comprehensive statute on personal data protection. While
its implementation is oriented towards the protection of individual
privacy rights, it imposes obligations to protect personal data on
controllers or processors, including Philippine government agencies who
act in such capacity. Failure to comply with these obligations may
result in the imposition not just of administrative, but also criminal
penalties. The threat of liability on government controllers and
processors has thus resulted in the embedding, whether formally or
unofficially, of data privacy justifications in denying access to or
withholding government data. Further, as the unnecessary retention of
personal data records increases the risk of liability for
controllers/processors under the Act, data privacy considerations have
also influenced data retention policies within the government.

Personal information must be retained only for as long as necessary for
the fulfillment of the purposes for which the data was obtained, for the
establishment, exercise or defense of legal claims, for legitimate
purposes or as provided by law.[^96] This, however, does not affect the
accessibility of information *per se* (i.e. it neither decreases nor
increases openness of the data to the public); it is merely a mandate to
retain for as long as necessary.

With respect to government data, NAP has published a General Records
Disposition Schedule common to all government agencies that lists the
retention periods of data depending on the type.

There is currently no law governing data localisation. However, DICT has
prescribed, through a circular, the Philippines Government’s Cloud First
Policy, which includes a provision on data residency.[^97] Section 12.2
of the circular as amended states that ‘as a general rule, no residency
restrictions shall be placed on government data stored or processed in
the cloud, provided that appropriate controls or security measures are
present’. By way of exception, the storage or processing of sensitive,
above-sensitive and highly-sensitive government data were made subject
to some residency restrictions.[^98] The data residency in such cases is
restricted to the Philippine territory or (only in the case of sensitive
government data) also to other states with which the Philippines has
enforceable extradition treaties for the turn-over of persons accused or
convicted of violating Philippine laws.[^99]

Data-sharing agreements across government agencies are further governed
by NPC Circular No. 16-02. The Implementing Rules to the DPA require
that any sharing of personal data for commercial purposes, or between
government agencies for a public function or provision of a public
service, be covered by a data sharing agreement. The data sharing
agreement must be in writing, and must contain mandatory provisions such
as the purpose of the agreement, the controllers party to the agreement,
the type of data to be shared, the processors who will have access to
the data, the designated data protection or compliance officer, the term
of the agreement, the operational details for the sharing or transfer of
personal data and the general description of security measures that
protect the personal data. Data sharing is allowed only where there are
adequate safeguards for data privacy and security, and any online access
granted by a government agency to personal data under its control or
custody must be done via a secure encrypted link.

##### Disclosures related to money laundering statutes

To protect and preserve the integrity and confidentiality of bank
accounts, the Philippines enacted the 2001 Anti-Money Laundering
Act.[^100] Under this Act, as amended, covered persons[^101] are
required to report covered and suspicious transactions to the AMLC. The
2018 Implementing Rules and Regulations of the Anti-Money Laundering Act
lists the following covered transactions: 1) a transaction in cash or
other equivalent monetary instrument exceeding 500,000 pesos
(PHP500,000.00); 2) a transaction with or involving jewelry dealers,
dealers in precious metals and dealers in precious stones in cash or
other equivalent monetary instrument exceeding 1,000,000 pesos
(PHP1,000,000); and 3) a casino cash transaction exceeding 5,000,000
pesos (PHP5,000,000) or its equivalent in other currency.[^102] In
addition, transactions that are considered suspicious or transactions
that were conducted or happened under suspicious circumstances[^103] are
also reported to AMLC.[^104] Suspicious transactions that are related to
terrorism financing, pursuant to the 2012 Terrorism Financing Prevention
and Suppression Act, are also reported to the AMLC[^105] .

Under the 2021 AMLC Registration and Reporting Guidelines, the Covered
Transaction Reports and Suspicious Transaction Reports are submitted in
electronic form and filed through AMLC’s File Transfer and Reporting
Facility.[^106] The guidelines also list the specific information
required to be included in the reports such as the transaction date and
time, transaction amount, nature and purpose of the transaction,
information about the parties involved and other participants (such as
counterparty, issuer, transactor, etc.) and details of suspicion, if
applicable.[^107]

##### Establishing and maintaining government digitised databases

Several laws or regulations impose standards and limitations, whether
technical or basic, in the collection, storage and processing of
government-held or controlled data. These standards and limitations,
insofar as they mandate or rationalise the organisation of government
data, generally operate to promote openness.

A common feature of these laws or regulations is the collation of
specific records into government-maintained databases. It is noticeable
that in recent years, statutes have become more explicit in requiring
that the specific information relevant to the particular statute,
whenever collected or processed by a government agency, be maintained in
a database. This can be attributed to a recognition of increased
digitalisation of government records, even if such digitalisation does
not necessarily lead to an explicit grant of access to the public of
such data.

###### Philippines ID system

One of the most significant data collection activities embarked upon by
the Philippine government is, through the 2017 Philippines
Identification System Act, the collection of personal information of all
Philippines citizens and resident aliens for the purpose of issuing an
official government-issued identification document known as the PhilID,
with the underlying information stored in a registry known as the
PhilSys Registry.[^108] The personal information collected covers a
person’s full name, sex, blood type, marital status, date and place of
birth, address, a front-facing photograph, fingerprint information, an
iris scan and other biometric information.[^109] Each registrant, as
well as a person upon birth, is assigned a randomly generated, unique
and permanent identification number.[^110] The resulting PhilID contains
a QR code with the fingerprint information and other security features
as safeguards for data privacy and security and the prevention of
falsified cards.[^111]

An individual’s record in the Registry is considered to be an official
and sufficient proof of identity,[^112] with the PhilID mandated by the
law to be honored and accepted, subject to authentication, in all
transactions requiring proof or verification of identity.[^113] With
this requirement, it is expected that more citizens would be able to
engage in transactions that require proof of identity, thus allowing for
greater financial inclusion. The greater engagement of citizens in such
transactions could potentially have the effect of increasing data flows
through the increased generation of data resulting from these
transactions.

###### CMBS database

Another notable recent legislated database was created through the 2018
Community-Based Monitoring System Act, a law that recognised that
effective poverty reduction programmes of the state would be greatly
enabled by a Community-based Monitoring System (CMBS) that ‘generates
updated and disaggregated data necessary in targeting beneficiaries,
conducting more comprehensive poverty analysis and needs prioritisation,
designing appropriate policies and interventions, and monitoring impact
over time.’[^114] The resulting CMBS as defined by law is ‘an organised
technology-based system of collecting, processing and validating
necessary disaggregated data that may be used for planning, programme
implementation and impact monitoring at the local level while empowering
communities to participate in the process.’ The data is collected at the
level of local governments (i.e. cities and municipalities), and
pertains to a ‘compendium of localised facts, figures, and maps on the
different dimensions of poverty such as health, nutrition, water,
sanitation, shelter, education, income, employment, security, and
participation’. The right to privacy though of respondents is recognised
as inviolable, with the participation of residents being purely
voluntary. The CMBS is instituted in every city and municipality, with
said local governments now required to have a statistician, whose
primary function is data collection, preservation, and safekeeping. Data
collection is required every three years, with the local governments
allowed to maintain their own CBMS database for use in local level
planning and programme implementation. The data collated is to be used
by appropriate national government agencies in prioritising timely,
relevant, and much-needed social protection programmes of government in
areas identified to have the highest incidence of poverty.

###### Law enforcement databases

A set of databases maintained by individual government agencies serve
the purpose of facilitating law enforcement and the investigation of
crimes. A database serving as a ‘modern NBI Clearance and Identification
Center’ is required under the 2016 NBI Reorganisation and Modernisation
Act to be established by the National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) for
civilian identification, including individual identifying marks,
fingerprints, and dental records, as well as all derogatory and criminal
records.[^115] The NBI is also required to maintain databases for
ballistic records of firearms, and a DNA databank.[^116] The
Anti-Terrorism Council is required under the 2020 Anti-Terrorism Act to
‘establish and maintain comprehensive database information systems on
terrorism, terrorist activities, and counter-terrorism
operations.’[^117] A government coordinating body tasked with enforcing
anti-trafficking and laws against the online sexual exploitation of
children is required to maintain a database (including such information
as fingerprints and complete criminal history) of all adults convicted
of sexual abuse or exploitation of children.[^118] The Land
Transportation Office is required under the 2019 Motorcycle Crime
Prevention Act[^119] to maintain and a database registry of motorcycles,
including details about the vehicles’ owners, to facilitate retrieval
for official investigation and law enforcement purposes; the general law
on motor vehicle registration enacted in 1964[^120] as well as the law
establishing the Land Transportation Office[^121] had been silent on the
maintenance of a general database or registry of all motorised land
vehicles even as it had required their registration. However, the LTO
was recently required under the 2022 Vintage Vehicle Regulation Act to
maintain a database of all operating “vintage vehicles” manufactured 40
or more years ago.[^122]

In connection with the enforcement of the Philippines money-laundering
law, the Anti-Money Laundering Council was authorised under the
Implementing Rules of the Anti-Money Laundering Act to establish an
information sharing system to enable the storing, tracking and analysis
of money laundering transactions for the prevention, detection and
investigation of money laundering offenses.[^123] The Council was thus
mandated to install a computerised system to be used in the creation and
maintenance of an information database.[^124] Philippines
money-laundering laws also impose requirements on private sector-held
data; covered institutions such as banks are required not only to report
so-called covered customer transactions that meet prescribed threshold
amounts, but also to maintain such records of transactions for at least
5 years.[^125] While the law itself was silent as to the standards to be
applied for the maintenance of these records, the Implementing Rules as
revised in 2018, required those a subset of covered person—those
considered as ‘complex’, - to adopt an electronic anti-money
laundering/counter-terrorism financing system capable of monitoring
risks associated with money laundering and terrorism financing, as well
as generating timely reports for the guidance and information of its
board of directors and senior management.[^126] The Rules specifically
require that such electronic system bear the following automated
functionalities:

> - Able to perform statistical analysis, profiling and able to detect
>   unusual patterns of account activity;
>
> - Able to check transfer parties and the existing customer database
>   for any listed undesirable individual or corporation;
>
> - Able to check for given names throughout the history of payment
>   stored in the system;
>
> - Able to generate all the covered transaction reports of the covered
>   person accurately;
>
> - Able to provide a complete audit trail;
>
> - Able to aggregate activities of a customer with multiple accounts on
>   a consolidated basis for monitoring and reporting purposes; and
>
> - Able to record all suspicious transactions and support the
>   investigation of alerts generated by the system and brought to the
>   attention of Senior Management whether or not a report was filed
>   with the AMLC.

##### SIM card registration information

The 2022 SIM Card Registration Act was enacted to provide law
enforcement agencies the tools to resolve crimes which involve the
utilisation of SIM cards used to empower cellular phone subscription
accounts.[^127] All end-users are required under the law to register
their SIMs with the public telecommunications entities providing the
account, utilising government-issued ID cards or similar identity
documents.[^128] The end-users are also to provide their full name, date
of birth, sex, and address, with the registration form accomplished
electronically through a platform or website to be provided by the
public telecommunications entities (PTEs).[^129] Access to the
information in the database maintained by the PTEs may be mandated by a
duly-issued subpoena by a competent authority pursuant to an
investigation based on a sworn complaint that a specific mobile number
was or is being used in the commission of a crime or that it was
utilised as a means to commit a malicious, fraudulent or unlawful act,
and that the complaint is unable to ascertain the identity of the
perpetrator.[^130]

#### Decreasing openness to favour individuals

##### Justified by protection of individual privacy rights

Several Philippines statutes impose confidentiality requirements
intended to protect the privacy rights of individuals. The statute on
personal data protection is the most comprehensive of these laws.

The 2012 DPA requires lawful basis for the processing of personally
identifying information, with the standards for lawful basis prescribed
in the law itself.[^131] The law also guarantees data subjects—any
natural person—several rights with respect to their personally
identifying information. The operative effects of these twin concepts
have resulted in significant limitations in the collection, sharing,
transfer and use of personal data, perhaps to an unprecedented extent
considering that proprietary rights to data itself had not been
well-defined up until the law’s passage.

The underlying premise of the 2012 DPA is the protection of an
individual’s constitutional right to privacy. The constitutional right
to privacy as recognised in the Philippines embodies several facets,
e.g. privacy attaching to certain locations such as one’s home
(locational privacy) and the right to make intimate personal decisions
without interference from the state. The right to privacy of one’s
personally identifying information was recognised by the Philippine
Supreme Court before the enactment of DPA, in its 1998 decision in *Ople
v. Torres*.[^132] Nonetheless, the subsequent law ushered in a more
definitive regime for the protection of personal data. Most crucially,
unlike the basic framework of the Bill of Rights where individual rights
are guaranteed protection against interference by state actors, data
privacy under the DPA protects one’s personal data privacy from
infringement by non-state actors, i.e. the private sector, to the extent
that there are now significant limitations on freedom of action by the
private actors who control the use of personally-identifying information
of other people.

With the DPA, one’s personally identifying information can only be
processed by another if there is lawful basis for processing, as
enumerated under Sections 12 and 13 of the Act, with heightened
restrictions for sensitive personal information. The consent to
processing by the data subject, in recorded form, is one of the grounds
for processing, and arguably the most prevalent one.

In understanding the effects of the DPA, lawful bases must also be seen
in conjunction with the rights of a data subject as enumerated in the
law. A data subject is capacitated by the law to invoke these rights
against any natural or juridical person who controls the processing of
the data subject’s information, towards the end of restricting if not
terminating the ability to process the personal information. Thus, any
public access rights or data sharing/transfer arrangements that the
controller may have enabled could be thwarted. Even the content of the
personal data may be altered or deleted due to the data subject’s
invocation of their rights.

Under the General Data Privacy Principles of the DPA, personal data must
be retained only for as long as necessary for the fulfillment of the
purposes for which the data was obtained or for the establishment,
exercise or defense of legal claims, or for legitimate business
purposes, or as provided by law.[^133] Thereafter, personal data should
be disposed of or discarded in a secure manner that would prevent
further processing, unauthorised access, or disclosure to any other
party or the public, or prejudice the interests of the data
subjects.[^134]

In addition, there was also a specific mention of the non-applicability
of the provisions on the rights of the data subject, transmissibility of
rights of the data subject and the right to data portability of the DPA
on data used only for needs of scientific and statistical research. Such
use, however, is combined with the responsibility that the data is held
under strict confidentiality and used only for the declared purpose. It
should be noted though that the Implementing Rules and Regulations of
the DPA seemingly added a specific requirement under this exception,
i.e. for data to be exempted from the application of DPA, the research
should be intended for a public benefit, \[and\] subject to the
requirements of applicable laws, regulations, or ethical
standards’.[^135]

With respect to cross-border data transfers, although there are no
specific restrictions, the NPC has addressed the matter of free flow of
data in relation to the DPA. It stated in an opinion that the DPA
‘concerns itself with the free flow of data but limited to the specific
context of personal data processing.’[^136] Transfer of personal data,
including cross-border transfers are expected to adhere to the general
privacy principles.[^137] Transfer of personal data (whether within or
across borders) would be considered as processing, which under the DPA
can only be permitted if it fulfills any of the criteria as enumerated
under Section 12 and Section 13 (for sensitive personal information) of
the law. Controllers are accountable and are expected to ensure that
‘recipients of personal data outside the Philippines process data in a
manner consistent with the requirements of the DPA and must put in place
contractual or other reasonable safeguards to guarantee a comparable
level of protection for data transferred.’[^138]

Other statutes also establish context-specific privacy rights in favor
of individuals.

The 2013 Philippines Statistical Act that implements the PhilSys ID
system classifies as privileged communication (and thus inadmissible as
evidence in any proceeding) individual data furnished by a respondent to
the PSA concerning statistical inquiries, surveys and census.[^139]
Information collected by the PSA in the implementation of the national
ID system under the 2017 PhilSys Act, is similarly covered under the
protection.[^140] SIM card registration information is also treated as
absolutely confidential under Section 9 of the 2022 SIM Card
Registration Act.[^141]

Other laws address the privacy concerns of individual members of
vulnerable sectors. The 2004 Violence Against Women and Their Children
Act imposes confidentiality requirements for all records pertaining to
cases of violence against women and their children, with violations of
confidentiality punishable with contempt of court.[^142] The 2022
Anti-Online Sexual Abuse and Exploitation of Children and Anti-Child
Sexual Abuse and Exploitation Materials Act similarly requires that any
record concerning a victim of child sexual abuse or exploitation be kept
confidential[^143] . The names of children alleged, accused of, or
adjudged as having committed a criminal offense under Philippine law are
required to be kept confidential under the 2006 Juvenile Justice and
Welfare Act.[^144] Confidentiality of persons with HIV or AIDS is
required under the 2018 Philippines HIV and AIDS Policy Act,[^145] and
for all relevant information related to persons with psychiatric,
neurologic and psychological health needs under the 2018 Mental Health
Act.[^146]

The 2008 Rule on the Writ of Habeas Data was promulgated by the
Philippine Supreme Court to provide a judicial remedy to persons whose
right to privacy in life, liberty or security is violated or threatened
by an unlawful act or omission, whether by a public official or
employee, a private individual or by an entity engaged in the gathering,
collecting or storing of data or information regarding the person,
family, home and correspondence of the aggrieved party.[^147] The
courts, through this writ, may, after deliberating on the petition,
order the deletion, destruction or rectification of the erroneous data
or information. The rule was enacted prior to the 2012 DPA, which offers
similar relief, though primarily through an administrative agency—NPC.
Nonetheless, the Rule on Habeas Data remains in force and available as a
remedy.

##### In favor of promoting intellectual property rights

Copyright is a legal protection afforded to literary, scholarly,
scientific, artistic works, and derivative works.[^148] It gives the
owner of the copyright the exclusive right to carry out, authorise or
prevent the following acts: a) reproduction of the work or substantial
portion of the work; b) dramatisation, translation, adaptation,
abridgment, arrangement or other transformation of the work; c) the
first public distribution of the original and each copy of the work by
sale or other forms of transfer of ownership; d) rental of the original
or a copy of an audiovisual or cinematographic work, a work embodied in
a sound recording, a computer programme, a compilation of data and other
materials or a musical work in graphic form, irrespective of the
ownership of the original or the copy which is the subject of the
rental; e) public display of the original or a copy of the work; f)
public performance of the work; and g) other communication to the public
of the work. In addition, Section 173.1(b) of the 1997 Intellectual
Property Code protects derivative works as new works, including
compilations of data and other materials which are original, by reason
of the selection, coordination or arrangement of their contents. This
protection does not affect the force of any subsisting copyright upon
the original works employed or any part thereof, nor can it be construed
to imply any right to such use of the original works, or to secure or
extend copyright in such original works.[^149] This provision on
derivative works is, so far, the only provision that might relate to web
scraping considering that currently, there are no specific laws or
regulations in the Philippines that intentionally regulate web scraping.
The works are generally protected by copyright during the lifetime of
the author and for 50 years after their death.[^150]

One of the limitations of the rights given to copyright owners is the
exception of fair use of copyrighted work from being considered as an
infringement of copyright.[^151] Section 185 of the 1997 Intellectual
Property Code states the fair use of a copyrighted work for criticism,
comment, news reporting, teaching including limited number of copies for
classroom use, scholarship, research, and similar purposes is not an
infringement of copyright. Under the 1997 Intellectual Property Code,
decompilation, which is understood to be the reproduction of the code
and translation of the forms of a computer programme to achieve the
interoperability of an independently created computer programme with
other programmes, may also constitute fair use, to the extent that such
decompilation is done for the purpose of obtaining the information
necessary to achieve interoperability of an independently created
computer programme with other programmes. In addition, the lawful owner
of a computer programme may create one (1) back-up copy or adaptation of
a computer programme, even without the authorisation of the author or
copyright owner, as long as such copy or adaptation is necessary for the
use of the computer programme in conjunction with a computer and for
archival or replacement purposes, in the event that the original
programme is lost or rendered unusable.[^152]

With respect to authorship, it should be noted though that the 1997
Intellectual Property Code does not recognise works that were purely
generated by artificial intelligence. Under the 1997 Intellectual
Property Code, copyright over original literary and artistic works
generally belong to the author, who under the law, is defined as a
‘natural person’, thus excluding non-humans, as well as juridical
persons.[^153] Currently, there are no laws or regulations in the
Philippines that specifically govern AI authorship.

Copyright over original literary and artistic works belong by default to
the author unless the work was created pursuant to a commission or in
the course of employment.[^154] Copyright may likewise be assigned, even
to a juridical person.[^155]

Independent of copyright, the author of a work also has ‘moral rights’,
which include the right of attribution, the withholding of a work from
publication and the prevention of any alteration or modification of the
work.[^156]

The Philippines protects undisclosed information or trade secrets. It is
listed in the Intellectual Property Code as one of the intellectual
property rights in accordance with the Agreement on Trade-Related
Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS Agreement).[^157] Under
the TRIPS Agreement, undisclosed information is protected. Undisclosed
information is defined as ‘any form or type of financial, business,
scientific, technical, economic or engineering information, including
the whole or any portion or phase of any process, procedure, formula,
improvement, pattern, plan, design, prototype, code, compilation,
programme, method, technique, or listing of names, addresses or
telephone numbers, whether tangible or intangible, stored, compiled, or
memorialised physically, electronically, graphically, photographically,
or in writing.’[^158] This protection includes being immune from modes
of discovery. Some noted exceptions to the rule on non-disclosure of
undisclosed information includes cases when courts may require the
disclosure of information ‘where it is indispensable for doing justice’
and in cases when other applicable laws for instance in cases regarding
consumer products, require it.[^159]

##### Data privacy and security

###### Online traffic data

The 2012 Cybercrime Prevention Act makes specific impositions on
entities that provide users the ability to communicate by means of a
computer system or those that process or store computer data on behalf
of such communications service (collectively, ‘service providers’) with
respect to ‘traffic data’, or any computer data other than the content
of the communication including, but not limited to, the communication’s
origin, destination, route, time, date, size, duration, or type of
underlying service.[^160] Service providers are required to preserve for
a period of at least six months such traffic data as well as the
subscriber information (including assigned network addresses) associated
with the traffic data.[^161] Such information may be disclosed to law
enforcement if authorised under a court warrant; the six-month
preservation period enables law enforcement to secure the information
necessary for a warrant.[^162]

Another provision of the 2012 Cybercrime Prevention Act, which would
have authorised law enforcement authorities to collect or record traffic
data in real time even without a warrant as long as there was ‘due
cause’, was struck down by the Philippine Supreme Court as
unconstitutional in the case of *Disini*.[^163] The court agreed with
the observation that ‘when seemingly random bits of traffic data are
gathered in bulk, pooled together and analysed, they reveal patterns of
activities which can then be used to create profiles of the persons
under surveillance’, with analysts then able to determine ‘a person’s
close associations, religious views, political affiliations, even sexual
preferences.’[^164] For that reason, the court held that the Cybercrime
Prevention Act should have been narrowly drawn to protect individual
rights, and that the legislated standard of ‘due cause’ was too general,
empowering law enforcement with unlimited authority that threatens an
individual’s right to privacy. Notably, the questioned provision
ultimately failed because the collection of traffic data was authorised
without needing to secure a court-issued search warrant. Nonetheless,
the court acknowledged that the Budapest Convention on Cybercrimes
requires signatory countries to adopt legislative measures to empower
state authorities to collect or record traffic data in real time; the
Philippines only acceded to the Budapest convention in 2018, or 4 years
after the Philippine Supreme Court’s decision in *Disini*.

###### Data security measures

The DPA requires personal data controllers, including the government, to
implement reasonable and appropriate organisational, physical, and
technical measures for the protection of personal data. For government
agencies that control the processing of personal information, the NPC
issued Circular No. 16-01 governing security of personal data in
government agencies. Section 6 of the circular recommends the use of the
ISO/IEC 27002 control set as the minimum standard to assess any gaps in
the agency’s control framework. In Section 12 of the circular, the NPC
recommends ISO/IEC 27018 as the most appropriate certification for the
service or function provided by a service provider that is engaged
either for the storage of data or as a personal information processor
acting under the control of the agency. These measures precede the Cloud
First Policy for government data issued by the DICT.

###### Private sector mandates relating to data protection measures

The relevant private sector mandates concerning standards generally
pertain to data or information security. The existence of these mandates
may have the effect of limiting the collection or processing of data,
especially if the compliance costs limit data processing by private
sector entities. However, the required security measures may also
promote openness as they better ensure the quality of data, thus
increasing confidence in their use.

***Applicable to all private sector entities***

The DPA requires personal data controllers, including the government, to
implement reasonable and appropriate organisational, physical, and
technical measures intended for the protection of personal data.[^165]
These measures are intended for the protection of personal information
against any accidental or unlawful destruction, alteration and
disclosure, as well as any other unlawful processing. A personal
information controller is required to implement such measures to protect
personal information against both natural dangers (such as accidental
loss or destruction) as well as human dangers. The need for such
security measures is reiterated in the implementing rules and
regulations of DPA[^166] , which requires an entity involved in the
processing of personal data to maintain records that sufficiently
describes their data processing system, including a general description
of the organisational, physical, and technical security measures in
place.[^167]

Notably, the detailed technical data security requirements issued by the
NPC for government agencies processing personal data, such as the
requirement for encryption of data and the recommended ISO/IEC 27018
standard, are not applicable to the private sector. The private sector
thus has more leeway in determining what data security measures they can
adopt with respect to personal data that they control or process.

Several categories of personal data are exempt from the scope of DPA.
These include ‘personal information processed for journalistic,
artistic, literary or research purposes’, thus enabling journalists
among others to perform their work without fear.[^168] However, the
exemption is qualified. Personal information processed for
journalistic,[^169] artistic or literary purposes is only exempted if it
is done ‘to uphold freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press,
\[and is\] subject to requirements of other applicable law or
regulations.’[^170] On the other hand, personal information processed
for research purposes is only exempt ’if it is intended for a public
benefit \[and is\] subject to the requirements of applicable laws,
regulations, or ethical standards.[^171]

Another important exemption relates to matters of public concern, such
as information about individuals employed by the government with respect
to their positions or functions, or information relating to
discretionary benefits of a financial nature given by the government to
an individual, or information about individuals performing services
under contract to the government that relates to the services
performed.[^172]

Yet another important category of exempted personal data relates to data
necessary to carry out the functions of public authority, such as law
enforcement or regulatory authorities who would necessarily need to
process personal information as they undertake their investigatory
functions.[^173] Information relevant to enforcement of banking and
money-laundering laws are similarly exempt.[^174]

***Applicable to specific private sector entities***

DICT, through Memorandum Circular No. 05 2017, has adopted rules and
regulations that apply to private sector entities whose computer systems
or networks are deemed as ‘Critical Information Infrastructure’
(CII)—systems deemed so vital to the country that interference with
them, their incapacitation or destruction would have a debilitating
impact on security, national or economic security, national health, or a
combination of such matters.[^175] Private sector entities that have
been classified as CIIs are those engaged in transportation, energy,
water, health, emergency services, banking and finance, business process
outsourcing, telecommunications and media.

CIIs are required to comply with the Philippines National Standard on
Information Security Management System ISO/IEC 27001.[^176] CIIs are
required to participate in an annual security assessment programme by
DICT that includes a security evaluation of operational systems based on
ISO/IEC TR 19791:2010.[^177] CIIs are also required to create their own
Computer Emergency Response Teams (CERT), and to secure from DICT a
Certificate of Cybersecurity Compliance based on criteria stipulated in
ISO/IEC 15408 and ISO 18045 as reference standards.

In 2020, through DICT Circular No. 10 2020,[^178] the Philippines Cloud
First Policy was expanded to cover not just government agencies, but
also ‘cloud service providers, intermediaries, and other private
entities with transactions, contracts, or data related to, in connection
with, or arising from the rendition of cloud computing services for the
Philippine government.’[^179] However, unlike government agencies, such
private entities are not required to adopt cloud computing as the
preferred ICT deployment strategy[^180] or to utilise the data
classification models prescribed by the policy for government
agencies.[^181] Such covered private sector entities are, by virtue of
the amended circular, subject to restrictions when processing government
data, including the recognition of the authority of the Philippine
government to determine the management, use, processing, storage,
security and accessibility of its data with sole control over the
encryption keys thereto.[^182]

###### Exceptions to data security for law enforcement

The Bill of Rights of the Philippine Constitution specifically
guarantees that ‘the privacy of communication and correspondence shall
be inviolable, except upon lawful order of the court, or when public
safety or order requires otherwise, as prescribed by law’. In relation
to this, several laws have provided specific circumstances when
interception of computer data by law enforcement authorities may be
allowed.

First, in connection with cybercrime offenses, the interception of
computer data by law enforcement authorities may be authorised by the
trial courts by virtue of a warrant to intercept computer data that
allows authorities to listen, record, monitor or surveil the content of
communications, including the procuring of content of computer data
through access and use of a computer system.[^183] The application for
the warrant must establish, among others, the existence of a probable
cybercrime offense and the communication or computer data that is sought
to be intercepted.

In connection with suspected terrorists or members of terrorist
organisations, the 2020 Anti-Terrorism Act allows for the Court of
Appeals to issue an order authorising law enforcement or military to
secretly wiretap, listen to, or intercept any private communications
between such suspects.[^184] The order may be issued by the Court of
Appeals upon determination that there is probable cause that any of the
crimes defined under the Anti-Terrorism Act has been, is being, or is
about to be committed.

In connection with offenses related to online sexual abuse, exploitation
of children, child sexual abuse or exploitation materials, the 2022
Anti-Online Sexual Abuse or Exploitation of Children (OSAEC) and
Anti-Child Sexual Abuse or Exploitation Materials (CSAEM) Act,[^185] law
enforcement officers may be authorised by a regional trial court to
track, intercept, view, monitor, surveil, listen to and record, by
technical or electronic means, any communications, information or
messages, including the procurement of content data, transmitted by
means of a computer system involving at least one person reasonably
believed to have committed violations under the said law.[^186] In
addition, the law specifically states that when the offense involves the
use of computer systems and digital platforms, a court order shall not
be required for a law enforcement officer acting in an undercover
capacity to intercept a communication with a person reasonably believed
to have committed, committing, or about to commit any of the violations
in the said law.[^187] In instances when an order is required, the
application must establish that: there are reasonable grounds to believe
that any of the crimes covered has been committed, is being committed,
or is about to be committed; there are reasonable grounds to believe
that evidence that will be obtained is essential to the conviction of
any person for, or to the solution of, or to the prevention of, any such
crime; and there are no other means readily available for obtaining such
evidence.[^188]

# Recommendations towards increasing data flows

The above survey of laws and policies establishes that despite the
absence thus far of any comprehensive data governance law, there is an
existing framework that takes into account the importance of generating
and preserving information for policy making and enforcement concerns,
the constitutional right to access to information, as well as other
constitutional concerns such as the right to privacy. A comprehensive
law will be expected to address these same concerns. It is also notable
that Philippines policy makers are increasingly cognisant that
digitisation and online access allow for more comprehensive data
collection and sharing.

However, certain real-world practices have been known to frustrate
access or sharing of data, even if such access or sharing is warranted
under the circumstances.

### Absence of a unified/centralised framework for inter-government sharing and providing open access to government data

The multiple individual government databases that implement the Open
Data Policy, notwithstanding there being an ostensibly centralised Open
Data website, highlights the fact that the sharing or granting of access
to data by individual government agencies is not a given, even if each
of these agencies belong to just one government. The fact that the Open
Data website is not even able to link to PSA’s more comprehensive data
sets all but highlights the failure for now, of the public-facing
aspects of the Open Data Policy.

This lack of inter-agency coordination and cooperation has also hampered
the expeditious implementation of the FOI programme. The researchers
conducted a key informant interview with a former ranking government
official who was tasked with administering the FOI programme until 2022.
He expressed frustration with other government agencies who, under
various justifications, would be hesitant to either share data even in
the context of fulfilling an FOI request. Such hesitance also accounts
for the failure of the Open Data Policy. In his opinion, the FOI
Ordinance is not sufficient. It is necessary to legislate an FOI statute
to compel the effective implementation of FOI. Indeed, the absence of an
FOI statute has resulted in more closed data flows. The President cannot
contravene confidentiality requirements imposed by other laws or by
jurisprudence even as they implement the right to access information on
public concerns, hence the many exceptions to the enforcement of the
current FOI Ordinance. Many of these confidentiality requirements were
imposed by laws from a different era with a different mindset about the
value of access to information, which was enshrined in the Philippine
Constitution in 1973. A new FOI law would afford Congress the
opportunity to revisit some of these exceptions in light of the
experience gained from the working of the ordinance, where the value of
information or data has become more clearly palpable. According to the
global rankings maintained by Open Data Watch, the Philippines has
declined in terms of open data from 18th out of 187 in 2020, to 37th out
of 197 in 2024. An FOI statute would help reverse such decline.

It is telling that existing laws or regulations may give hesitant
government agencies colourable legal justification to withhold
government data, even if requested by other government agencies. For
example, the statutory mandate of PSA plainly establishes that agency’s
role as the primary collator and processor of government statistical
information. Data collection and processing undertaken by other
government agencies, in contrast, are merely incidental to the
performance of their respective statutory mandates. The primary mandate
of PSA when it comes to statistical data, thus, gives it authority not
only to implement autonomous open data access policies or platforms, but
also to refuse interconnection or sharing with other agencies on the
ground that its own mandate would be diminished.

Another statute that has operated to inhibit data sharing within
government is the DPA. The Act has been commonly invoked towards the end
of denying access to information. At times, the invocation can be
misplaced, such as when it is intended to shield government data that
cannot be used to identify specific individuals and is hence outside the
scope of the Act.[^189]

Moreover, the law itself acknowledges the right to access of information
on matters of public concern, thus exempting from its scope personal
information about public officials relating to their public functions,
or those performing services under government contracts or about
recipients of discretionary benefits of a financial nature.[^190] The
law also excludes information necessary to carry out functions of public
authorities such as law enforcement agencies.[^191] However, Sections 25
of the law also imposes criminal penalties including prison time for
unauthorised processing of personal information, an act that includes
disclosures of personal information that violate the law. Section 36
also provides that if the convicted offender is a public officer, they
would be disqualified from occupying public office for a term, double
the term of the criminal penalty. The prospect of these twin punishments
has resulted in public officers being extremely conservative in
disclosing personal information, even if outside the scope of the Act
and even if clearly within the scope of the right to access to
information on matters of public concern. The same fear has also
deterred easy access to civil registry and land registry records.
Instead, one who is denied the right to access such information is
forced to resort to initiating prolonged judicial proceedings. The
removal of these criminal penalties under DPA, and confining punishments
to administrative fines, would inevitably lead public custodians of data
to be more liberal than they are right now in disclosing information.

Whether justified by exposure to legal liability or by relatively
trivial concerns about agency diminution, the clear absence of a data
sharing mandate within government hampers the ability of different
agencies to adopt data-based policy making that ensures that decisions
on public issues and services are made based on evidence, expertise, and
science. Legislated statutes may address the gap, but it is already
possible for executive action, through an executive order, to require
data sharing and prescribe any necessary safeguards for the protection
of individual rights such as that of privacy. The President is
recognised by the Constitution as having control of all the executive
departments, bureaus, and offices.[^192] By virtue of such authority,
the President may require inter-agency sharing of information; the FOI
Statute already stands as one standing example, as does the 1993
Executive Order No. 53, which directs all government agencies to provide
the national taxation authorities with the necessary information to help
increase tax collections. The President could even revive the apparently
moribund PeGIF initiative, which would have required individual
government agencies to adopt common standard definitions for the purpose
of ensuring interoperability of data. The fact that the PeGIF initiative
has not moved forward is an indication of the low priority within the
executive branch of implementing open data and inter-agency sharing of
information.

### Increasing access to traffic data

The restrictions imposed on the use by law enforcement of internet
traffic data (traffic data), with the Supreme Court imposed requirement
of a judicial warrant, through *Disini*, has also hampered the
investigation of cybercrimes by law enforcement. The collection of
traffic data is often but the first step in the investigation of
cybercrimes, especially since most cybercriminals are canny enough to
take steps to hide under the cloak of anonymity as they undertake their
online criminal activity. Without traffic data, law enforcement would
usually be unable to even identify which online accounts or IP addresses
are involved in the commission of cybercrimes.

The Budapest Convention on Cybercrime itself recognises the unique
nature of traffic data. It distinguishes traffic data from either
content data, subscriber information, or stored computer data. Traffic
data, which is non-identifying in nature, is treated distinctly under
the Convention with the requirement for its expedited preservation and
expedited partial disclosure. The Convention requires State parties to
ensure the ‘expeditious disclosures’ to a competent authority of a
sufficient amount of traffic data, to enable the identification of
service providers and the path through which the communication was
transmitted, and to adopt legislative and other measures to empower
competent authorities to compel service providers to collect or record
traffic data in real-time or to themselves collect or record through
technical means such traffic data.

The Convention does not require a judicial warrant to obtain traffic
data; it recognises that a ‘production order’ may be required for
computer data or subscriber information, but not for traffic data.
However, a ‘production order’ or warrant is now required in the
Philippines on account of the *Disini* decision. A search warrant in the
Philippines calls for the application of Section 2, Article III,
otherwise known as the Bill of Rights of the Philippine Constitution,
which requires that an application for a search warrant be granted only
upon ‘probable cause’, to be determined personally by a judge after
examination under oath of the complainant and the witnesses they may
produce. The Supreme Court itself has implemented the warrant
requirement it prescribed through the 2018 Rule on Cybercrime Warrants.
The Rules do allow law enforcement authorities to, without need of
judicial intervention, direct service providers to preserve traffic data
for a period of six months from the date of transaction.[^193] However,
law enforcement is not able to access such preserved traffic data
without obtaining from a court a Warrant to Disclose Computer Data
(WDCD). The WDCD is also resorted to for law enforcement to obtain
subscriber information and content data in the possession of service
providers, notwithstanding the differentiated treatment of these
different data categories under the Budapest Convention.

The application for a court-issued WDCD requires law enforcement to
identify several pieces of information that satisfy the Constitution’s
probable cause requirement, probable cause requiring ‘such facts and
circumstances that would lead a reasonably prudent man to believe that
an offense has been committed and the objects sought in connection with
that offense are in the place to be searched.’[^194] The Rules on
Cybercrime Warrants conflate the warrant requirements for traffic data,
content data and subscriber information even, if the Budapest Convention
differentiates all three. These burdensome requirements were
necessitated by the inadequacies perceived by the Supreme Court over the
2012 Cybercrime Prevention Act when it decided *Disini* in 2014.
However, it can be argued that a supervening event has since taken
place—the accession by the Philippines to the Budapest Convention in
2018. The Convention was transformed into part of the law of the
Philippines following its ratification by the Philippine Senate in
February 2018.

The provisions of the Budapest Convention have since become binding on
the Philippines. Thus, the Philippines obliges itself to ‘ensure the
expeditious disclosure of a sufficient amount of traffic data’, to
enable the identification of service providers and the path through
which the communication was transmitted. Arguably, the WDCD and the
constitutional necessity to produce probable cause do not serve the
obligation to ‘ensure the expeditious disclosure of traffic data’. At
the same time, because such obligations were not yet binding on the
Philippines when *Disini* was decided, the applicable legal landscape
that the Supreme Court needed to grapple with at that time has been
altered in present day.

The *Disini* decision was especially concerned that the disclosure of
traffic data was not subject to judicial authorisation. It can be argued
that the applicable procedural rules can be duly modified to address
both the Supreme Court’s concerns and the extant obligations created by
Article 17 of the Budapest Convention. There are judicial processes
short of a warrant, that are already available in the Philippines to
compel the production of evidence, such as a subpoena which does not
require the prior establishment of probable cause as required by the
Constitution, subjected as it is by a mere determination of relevance to
the issue subject of the controversy.[^195] The judicial oversight that
the Supreme Court determined was necessary to ensure that individual
privacy concerns are not overridden, can be established through a
subpoena-like process.

Notably, the Budapest Convention obligates State parties to enact
legislative ‘and other measures’ to implement Article 17. It would not
be necessary and would even run counter to the Philippine Constitution
for a resort to legislative measures. The Constitution vests on the
Supreme Court the exclusive power to ‘promulgate rules concerning the
protection and enforcement of constitutional rights, pleading, practice
and procedure in all courts.’[^196] Only the Supreme Court may enact
procedural rules, including rules governing the issuance of both
warrants and subpoenas. Thus, the expeditious measures required under
the Budapest Convention, should they involve judicial intervention
consistent with *Disini*, would be within the exclusive province of the
Supreme Court to undertake.

With the current judicial warrant requirement, which involves a judicial
determination first of probable cause, the expediency that the
Convention alludes to is often mooted. The freeing up of traffic data
for purposes of allowing law enforcement to comprehensively investigate
cybercrimes is deemed as a necessity.

These two concerns about the absence of a centralised data sharing
framework within government and the impositions when it comes to traffic
data ultimately highlight that in the Philippines, more serious and
elevated discussions are still necessary to underscore the necessity and
value of data as a useful tool for governance and policy administration.
The intangibility of data defies common perceptions as to what is
property and therefore results in a greater lack of care or concern
toward this increasingly valuable public asset. A greater realisation by
the State of the importance and indispensable value of data would be a
necessary first step for a more orderly and rational data governance
framework.
